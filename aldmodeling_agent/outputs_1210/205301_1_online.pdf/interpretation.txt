This model describes how an ALD precursor A transports into a high‑aspect‑ratio feature and reacts with the surface until saturation. It couples:

- Gas‑phase and Knudsen diffusion → effective diffusion \(D_{\text{eff}}\)
- Langmuir‑type adsorption/desorption → coverage \(\theta(t,x)\)
- A penetration front \(x_s(t)\) that separates reacted from unreacted regions
- Geometric effects via \(H, W,\) and the hydraulic diameter \(h\)

I’ll walk through each part and link the variables using the given dependency chains.

---

## 1. Gas‑phase diffusion, Knudsen diffusion, and \(D_{\text{eff}}\)

### 1.1 Molecular (gas‑phase) diffusion \(D_A\)

Molecular diffusion arises from gas–phase collisions (A–A and A–B). It is built from:

1. **Molecular thermal speed**
\[
v_A = \left(\frac{8RT}{\pi M_A}\right)^{1/2}
\]
- Depends on **molar mass** \(M_A\) and **temperature** \(T\).
- \(M_A \uparrow \Rightarrow v_A \downarrow\), \(T \uparrow \Rightarrow v_A \uparrow\).

2. **Collision term \(z_A\)** (dimension of time; effectively a “collision parameter”):
\[
z_A = \frac{\pi}{4} (d_A + d_B)^2 
\left[ \frac{8RT}{\pi} \left( \frac{1}{M_A} + \frac{1}{M_B} \right) \right]^{1/2} 
\frac{p_B}{RT} 
+ \tau (d_A)^2 
\left[ \frac{16RT}{\pi M_A} \right]^{1/2} 
\frac{p_A}{RT}
\]
- Depends on molecular diameters \(d_A,d_B\), molar masses \(M_A,M_B\), pressures \(p_A,p_B\), and a factor \(\tau\).
- Encodes the effect of **binary** and **self‑collisions** on diffusion.

3. **Molecular diffusion coefficient**
\[
D_A = \frac{3\pi}{16}\,v_A^2\,z_A
\]
Dependency chain:
\[
M_A, T, d_A, d_B, p_A, p_B, \tau \;\rightarrow\; v_A, z_A \;\rightarrow\; D_A
\]

Physically: higher \(T\) or lower \(M_A\) increases \(v_A\), and depending on collision environment (through \(z_A\)) you get a larger/smaller \(D_A\).

---

### 1.2 Knudsen diffusion \(D_{Kn}\)

In narrow features where molecule–wall collisions dominate, Knudsen diffusion is important. It is set by the **hydraulic diameter** \(h\) and molecular speed:

1. **Hydraulic diameter of a rectangular channel**
\[
h = \frac{1}{2} \frac{H W}{H+W}
\]
- Geometry only: depends on **height** \(H\) and **width** \(W\).
- For a narrow, tall trench, \(h \approx \tfrac{1}{2}\min(H,W)\).

2. **Knudsen diffusion coefficient**
\[
D_{Kn} = h \left(\frac{8RT}{9\pi M_A}\right)^{1/2}
\]

Dependency chain:
\[
H, W \rightarrow h \rightarrow D_{Kn}
\]
plus \(M_A, T\) for the thermal speed factor.

Interpretation: making the trench narrower (smaller \(H\) or \(W\) → smaller \(h\)) reduces \(D_{Kn}\), i.e. transport is more restricted by wall collisions.

---

### 1.3 Effective diffusion \(D_{\text{eff}}\)

In real ALD conditions, both molecular and Knudsen mechanisms act. They combine as **resistances in series**:

\[
D_{\text{eff}} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{D_A}+\dfrac{1}{D_{Kn}}}
\]

- If \(D_A \gg D_{Kn}\), then \(D_{\text{eff}} \approx D_{Kn}\) (Knudsen‑limited).
- If \(D_{Kn} \gg D_A\), then \(D_{\text{eff}} \approx D_A\) (molecular‑diffusion‑limited).

Key chain:
\[
M_A,T,d_A,\ldots \rightarrow D_A,\; H,W \rightarrow h \rightarrow D_{Kn}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad D_{\text{eff}}(M_A,T,d_A,H,W,\ldots)
\]

---

### 1.4 Molecular size \(d_A\) and its role

\(d_A\) is not taken as an arbitrary parameter; it’s inferred from material properties:

1. From kinetic theory:
\[
d_A \approx \sqrt{\frac{5}{16 \pi N_0} \sqrt{\frac{M_A R T}{\pi}}}
\]

2. From condensed‑phase density:
\[
d_A \approx 2\left(\frac{3M_A}{4\pi\rho N_0}\right)^{1/3}
\]

So:
\[
N_0,M_A,R,T,\rho \rightarrow d_A \rightarrow z_A \rightarrow D_A \rightarrow D_{\text{eff}}
\]

This connects macroscopic quantities (molar mass, density) to transport through an effective hard‑sphere diameter.

---

## 2. Transport equation for precursor pressure \(p_A(x,t)\)

The 1D model along the feature depth \(x\) is:

\[
\frac{\partial p_A}{\partial t} = D_{\text{eff}}\frac{\partial^2 p_A}{\partial x^2} 
- g\frac{4RT}{h\,N_0}
\]

- **Diffusion term**: \(D_{\text{eff}}\partial^2 p_A/\partial x^2\) accounts for gas‑phase transport along \(x\).
- **Sink term**: \(g\,4RT/(hN_0)\) couples to surface reaction:
  - \(g = f_{\text{ads}} - f_{\text{des}}\) is the **net adsorption flux** per unit area.
  - The factor \(4RT/(hN_0)\) converts surface flux (mol/m²s) on the walls into an effective **volumetric consumption** (Pa/s) in the channel of hydraulic diameter \(h\).

Dependency chain:
\[
D_{\text{eff}}, g, h, N_0,R,T \rightarrow \partial p_A/\partial t
\]

### Quasi‑steady (diffusion dominated) approximation

If transport adjusts much faster than surface coverage changes, we often set:

\[
D_{\text{eff}}\frac{\partial^2 p_A}{\partial x^2} \approx 0
\]

→ yields a **linear** pressure profile in the region where A is present:

\[
p_A(x,t) = p_{A0}\left(1-\frac{x}{x_s}\right), \quad x < x_s
\]

- \(p_{A0}\): inlet pressure at \(x=0\)
- \(x_s\): **penetration depth** (front position) where \(p_A \to 0\) or effectively no A beyond.

So:
\[
D_{\text{eff}}, x_s \rightarrow p_A(x)
\]

The physical picture: gas diffuses in, gets consumed by adsorption, and a moving front \(x_s(t)\) marks the limit of significant coverage growth during a half‑cycle.

---

## 3. Diffusive flux \(F(x,t)\)

The molar flow of A through a cross‑section:

\[
F(x,t) = - H W \frac{D_{\mathrm{eff}}}{k_B T} \frac{d p_A}{d x}
\]

Using the linear profile,
\[
p_A(x) = p_{A0}\left(1-\frac{x}{x_s}\right) \Rightarrow \frac{d p_A}{d x} = -\frac{p_{A0}}{x_s}
\]
so
\[
F(x,t) \approx H W \frac{D_{\mathrm{eff}}}{k_B T} \frac{p_{A0}}{x_s}
\]

(Your compact expression has \(\rho_A\) but conceptually it’s the same scaling: \(F \propto HW D_{\text{eff}} / x_s\).)

- **Scales with geometry** via cross‑section \(HW\).
- **Scales inversely with penetration depth** \(x_s\): the deeper the front, the smaller the gradient \(|dp_A/dx|\), so for a fixed inlet pressure you get lower flux per length.

Dependency chain:
\[
H,W,D_{\text{eff}},T,x_s\rightarrow F(x,t)
\]

---

## 4. Langmuir‑type adsorption, desorption, and coverage \(\theta\)

### 4.1 Adsorption flux \(f_{\text{ads}}\)

\[
f_{\text{ads}} = (1-\theta)\frac{c N_0 p_A}{\sqrt{2\pi M_A RT}}
\]

- \(p_A\): local gas‑phase partial pressure.
- \(c\): **sticking probability** (fraction of collisions that stick).
- \((1-\theta)\): fraction of free sites.
- Denominator: proportional to mean molecular velocity → flux of impinging molecules.

Dependencies:
\[
p_A, \theta, c, N_0, M_A, R, T \rightarrow f_{\text{ads}}
\]

Behavior:
- Increases with \(p_A\) and \(c\).
- Decreases as surface fills (\(\theta\to 1\)).
- Temperature: higher \(T\) increases molecular flux (tends to increase \(f_{\text{ads}}\)), but in a more complete model it can also reduce sticking or increase desorption; here \(c\) is taken constant.

---

### 4.2 Site capacity \(q\) and desorption flux \(f_{\text{des}}\)

The **site density per area**:

\[
q = \frac{b_{\text{film}}\,\rho\,g P C_{\text{sat}}}{b_A\,M}\,N_0
\]

Here \(q\) ties material/film parameters to how many A‑sites per m² exist. Important dependencies:
\[
\rho, g, b_A, M, N_0 \rightarrow q
\]
(plus the thickness per cycle, stoichiometric factor, etc., encoded in \(b_{\text{film}}, gP, C_{\text{sat}}\).)

Desorption is taken as a simple proportional law:

\[
f_{\text{des}} = q P_0 d_s
\]

- \(P_0d_s\) is an effective desorption attempt rate times probability.
- Scales linearly with \(q\): more sites → more potential desorption.

Dependencies:
\[
q \rightarrow f_{\text{des}}
\]

---

### 4.3 Net adsorption \(g\) and coverage evolution

Net rate:
\[
g = f_{\text{ads}} - f_{\text{des}}
\]

Coverage dynamics:
\[
\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \frac{g}{q}
\]

Chain:
\[
p_A,\theta,c,N_0,M_A,R,T \rightarrow f_{\text{ads}}
\]
\[
q \rightarrow f_{\text{des}}
\]
\[
f_{\text{ads}},f_{\text{des}} \rightarrow g \rightarrow \frac{d\theta}{dt}
\]

Interpretation:

- When \(f_{\text{ads}} > f_{\text{des}}\): \(g>0\) → \(\theta\) increases toward saturation.
- When \(f_{\text{ads}} = f_{\text{des}}\): \(g=0\) → \(\theta\) has reached a local equilibrium \(\theta_{\text{eq}}\).
- \(q\) rescales how a given flux \(g\) translates into a change in fractional coverage: larger \(q\) (more sites per area) → slower change of \(\theta\) for the same flux.

---

### 4.4 Equilibrium and Langmuir constant \(K\)

At equilibrium, \(g=0\):
\[
f_{\text{ads}}(\theta_{\text{eq}},p_A) = f_{\text{des}}(\theta_{\text{eq}})
\]

From the Langmuir form you get:
\[
K = \frac{\theta_{\text{eq}}/p_A}{1-\theta_{\text{eq}}}
\]

In terms of microscopic parameters:
\[
Q = \frac{N_0}{\sqrt{2\pi M_A RT}}
\]
\[
K = \frac{c Q}{q P_0 d_s}
\]

So:
\[
N_0,M_A,R,T \rightarrow Q
\]
\[
c,Q,q,P_0,d_s \rightarrow K
\]

And via \(\theta_{\text{eq}}\):
\[
K, p_A \rightarrow \theta_{\text{eq}}
\]

Physically: large \(K\) (large \(c\), small \(qP_0d_s\)) means strong adsorption: high \(\theta_{\text{eq}}\) even at small \(p_A\). Small \(K\) means weak adsorption.

---

## 5. Penetration depth \(x_s\) and saturation profile

### 5.1 Definition and role of \(x_s\)

\(x_s\) is the **saturation (or reaction) front position**:

- For \(x < x_s\): gas A penetrates, \(p_A(x) > 0\), and \(\theta(x,t)\) can grow.
- For \(x > x_s\): essentially no gas A, \(p_A \approx 0\), and \(\theta\) remains at its previous value (for that cycle).

The pressure profile for \(x < x_s\):
\[
p_A(x,t) = p_{A0}\left(1-\frac{x}{x_s}\right)
\]

Thus:
\[
D_{\text{eff}}, x_s, p_{A0} \rightarrow p_A(x)
\]
\[
p_A(x) \rightarrow f_{\text{ads}}(x) \rightarrow g(x) \rightarrow \theta(x,t)
\]

### 5.2 How \(x_s\) emerges

While not explicitly written as an ODE for \(x_s\) here, conceptually:

1. The integrated consumption of A by adsorption up to \(x_s\) must match the incoming diffusive flux \(F(0)\) over the exposure time.
2. The faster the surfaces saturate (larger \(g\) and smaller \(q\) → faster \(\theta \to \theta_{\text{eq}}\)), the **shorter** the penetration for a given pulse time.
3. Larger \(D_{\text{eff}}\) allows gas to penetrate deeper before being fully consumed, giving a larger \(x_s\).

So:
\[
D_{\text{eff}},c,K,q,\text{pulse time} \;\Rightarrow\; x_s
\]

In practice: higher \(D_{\text{eff}}\) or longer pulse → deeper \(x_s\); stronger adsorption (larger \(K\) or \(c\)) or larger capacity \(q\) → more rapid consumption per length, so \(x_s\) grows more slowly with time.

---

## 6. Geometry: \(H, W, h\) and aspect ratio

Geometry enters in several ways:

1. **Hydraulic diameter for Knudsen diffusion**
   - \(h = \frac{1}{2}\frac{HW}{H+W}\)
   - Narrower dimension reduces \(h\), decreases \(D_{Kn}\), and thus can limit \(D_{\text{eff}}\).

2. **Cross‑section scaling of flux**
   - \(F \propto H W D_{\text{eff}} / x_s\)
   - Larger cross‑section \(HW\) allows more total molar flow given the same gradient.

3. **Reaction sink term**
   - The volumetric sink in the PDE is \(g \cdot 4RT / (h N_0)\).
   - Smaller \(h\) (narrower channels) → stronger effective sink per unit volume, because surfaces are closer and dominate the volume.

If the feature length is \(L\):

- The **aspect ratio** is \(\text{AR} = L/\min(H,W)\).
- For high AR, getting \(x_s \approx L\) (full penetration) becomes difficult because:
  - Diffusion path is long.
  - Walls are close (small \(h\)), enhancing Knudsen resistance and sink strength.

So high AR + narrow \(H\) or \(W\) → small \(D_{\text{eff}}\), strong sink → \(x_s \ll L\) unless pulses are long or pressure is high.

---

## 7. Distinguishing key symbols

To clarify the specific meanings:

- \(D_A\): **Molecular diffusion coefficient** (gas‑phase, collision‑limited).
- \(D_{Kn}\): **Knudsen diffusion coefficient** (wall‑collision‑limited).
- \(D_{\text{eff}}\): **Effective diffusion coefficient**, combining both:
  \[
  \frac{1}{D_{\text{eff}}} = \frac{1}{D_A} + \frac{1}{D_{Kn}}
  \]

- \(g\): **Net adsorption rate per area**:
  \[
  g = f_{\text{ads}} - f_{\text{des}}
  \]
  Appears:
  - In surface coverage evolution (\(d\theta/dt = g/q\)).
  - As a sink in the gas‑phase diffusion equation.

- \(g_{pc}\) (often used in literature but not explicitly in your list) typically denotes **growth per cycle** (thickness). In your equations, this role is encoded in the composite factors in \(q\). If you see \(g\) multiplied by film factors, that’s a mapping from surface coverage to film thickness.

- \(K\): **Langmuir equilibrium constant**:
  \[
  K = \frac{\theta_{\text{eq}}/p_A}{1-\theta_{\text{eq}}}
  = \frac{c Q}{q P_0 d_s}
  \]
  Links macroscopic equilibrium behavior to microscopic adsorption/desorption parameters.

- \(Q\): **Kinetic prefactor**:
  \[
  Q = \frac{N_0}{\sqrt{2\pi M_A RT}}
  \]
  Essentially the collision flux normalization that appears in adsorption.

- \(q\): **Site density per area** (max number of A species per unit area). Converts **molecular flux** \(g\) to **coverage rate** \(d\theta/dt\).

---

## 8. Putting it all together: coupling and influence chains

To summarize how everything interacts:

1. **Transport side**
   - \(M_A, T, d_A, H, W \rightarrow D_A, D_{Kn} \rightarrow D_{\text{eff}}\)
   - \(D_{\text{eff}}\) and geometry define how quickly and how far gas can diffuse:
     \[
     D_{\text{eff}}, H, W \rightarrow F(x,t), \quad D_{\text{eff}},g,h \rightarrow \partial p_A/\partial t
     \]

2. **Surface kinetics side**
   - Material & film parameters:
     \[
     \rho, g_{\text{film}}, b_A, M, N_0 \rightarrow q
     \]
   - Gas and surface parameters:
     \[
     c, M_A, T, p_A, \theta \rightarrow f_{\text{ads}}
     \]
     \[
     q,P_0,d_s \rightarrow f_{\text{des}}
     \]
   - Then:
     \[
     f_{\text{ads}}, f_{\text{des}} \rightarrow g \rightarrow d\theta/dt
     \]

3. **Equilibrium thermodynamics**
   - From microscopic:
     \[
     N_0,M_A,R,T \rightarrow Q,\quad c,Q,q,P_0,d_s \rightarrow K
     \]
   - From macroscopic equilibrium:
     \[
     K, p_A \rightarrow \theta_{\text{eq}}
     \]

4. **Penetration depth and saturation**
   - \(p_A(x)\) is shaped by \(D_{\text{eff}}\) and \(x_s\).
   - \(p_A(x)\) feeds into local \(f_{\text{ads}}(x)\), \(g(x)\), and thus \(\theta(x,t)\).
   - The condition that the incoming flux is balanced by integrated consumption in the accessible domain sets \(x_s(t)\), hence the final penetration depth for a given pulse.

Overall:

- Stronger diffusion (large \(D_{\text{eff}}\)), higher pressure \(p_{A0}\), and longer pulse → larger \(x_s\) and more uniform coverage.
- Stronger adsorption (large \(K\), large \(c\)) or larger site capacity \(q\) → more rapid uptake per unit length → gas is depleted shallower → smaller \(x_s\) for a given pulse, i.e. stronger front‑like behavior.
- Narrower geometry (small \(H,W\)) → smaller \(h\), smaller \(D_{Kn}\), thus smaller \(D_{\text{eff}}\) and larger effective reaction sink → lower penetration and stronger gradients.

This is how the model simultaneously captures gas‑phase/Knudsen transport, Langmuir adsorption/desorption, penetration depth, and geometry effects in ALD growth in high‑aspect‑ratio structures.
